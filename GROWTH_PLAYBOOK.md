# CodeSimplr: First 10 Sales Playbook

This is the working acquisition plan for reaching the first 10 attributable sales. The first campaign focuses on one audience so the message, proof, follow-up, and learning loop stay coherent.

## Primary audience

Founder-led recruitment and staffing agencies, initially in the USA, UK, and Canada, with roughly 5–50 employees.

Strong fit signals:

- The agency is actively hiring or serving several open roles.
- Candidate intake, screening, follow-up, or client reporting still involves repeated manual work.
- The team uses several disconnected forms, inboxes, spreadsheets, ATS, or CRM tools.
- A founder, operations lead, or recruiting lead is visible and reachable through a business channel.
- The website does not clearly explain how candidate and client workflows are automated.

Exclude job seekers, firms with no visible workflow need, scraped personal addresses, anyone who has opted out, and contacts who cannot reasonably influence the buying decision.

## Offer and path to a sale

1. Offer a free 3-point recruitment workflow audit.
2. Return three specific priorities across candidate intake, follow-up, and reporting.
3. Recommend the fixed-scope 14-day paid pilot only when a useful implementation is evident.
4. Agree scope, commercial terms, success criteria, and owner before counting a sale.

A sale is counted only when a paid engagement is accepted and payment has been received. The standard first engagement covers one workflow, one primary input, one destination system, one human approval point, testing, documentation, and handover over 14 days. Set one price and payment schedule before outreach begins; do not improvise a different offer for every lead.

## Research checklist

Spend two or three minutes validating each prospect before contacting them:

- Agency niche and location
- One visible workflow signal or friction point
- Relevant decision-maker and business contact method
- The exact sentence that makes the opening message specific
- Source URL and date reviewed

Never invent a problem, customer result, relationship, or compliment. If there is no real observation, skip the prospect.

## Email sequence

Keep the same thread. Stop on reply or opt-out.

### Email 1 — observation and question

Subject: `A workflow question for {{agency_name}}`

Hi {{first_name}},

I noticed {{specific, verifiable observation about their recruiting workflow, jobs, or site}}.

When candidate volume rises, is {{intake / follow-up / reporting}} still handled manually by your team, or have you already connected it to your ATS/CRM?

I build practical recruiting workflows that reduce repetitive admin while keeping recruiters in control. I can send a free 3-point audit for {{agency_name}} if useful: {{campaign_link}}

Best,  
Sparsh  
CodeSimplr

P.S. If this is not relevant, reply “no” and I will not follow up.

### Email 2 — useful example, 3–4 business days later

Subject: `Re: A workflow question for {{agency_name}}`

Hi {{first_name}},

One common starting point is connecting a candidate form to structured screening notes, an ATS/CRM update, and a human-approved follow-up. It is small enough to pilot without replacing the systems the team already trusts.

Would a one-page map of where that could help {{agency_name}} be useful? No meeting is required for the audit.

Best,  
Sparsh

### Email 3 — close the loop, 5–7 business days later

Subject: `Should I close this out?`

I have not heard back, so I will close this out. If reducing manual recruiting admin becomes a priority, the 3-point audit is here: {{campaign_link}}

Best,  
Sparsh

## LinkedIn sequence

Connection note:

> Hi {{first_name}} — I noticed {{specific agency signal}}. I work on practical candidate-intake and follow-up automation for recruiting teams. Happy to connect; no pitch needed.

After acceptance, wait and engage normally before sending a relevant note:

> Thanks for connecting. Quick question: where does your team feel the most repeated admin today—candidate intake, follow-up, or client reporting? I am mapping a few practical patterns for agencies and can share a 3-point audit if it would help.

Do not copy the email sequence into LinkedIn. Keep messages conversational, stop when there is no interest, and do not automate personal-account activity that violates the platform’s rules.

## Organic social plan

Publish three useful posts per week:

1. A short teardown of one recruiting workflow, with no client names or confidential data.
2. A before/after process map showing what remains human and what becomes automated.
3. A practical checklist answering one objection, such as data quality, ATS compatibility, or keeping recruiter judgment in the loop.

Post call to action:

> If candidate intake or follow-up is becoming repetitive, I will map three practical improvements for your current workflow. The free audit is here: {{organic_social_link}}

## Referrals and messaging channels

Ask five relevant existing contacts or partners per week for a specific introduction:

> I am helping recruitment and staffing teams reduce repetitive candidate-intake and follow-up work. Do you know one agency founder or operations lead who is actively dealing with that? An introduction would be appreciated; I will keep it useful and low-pressure.

Use WhatsApp only for inbound enquiries, existing relationships, or explicit opt-in. Do not buy lists or send unsolicited bulk messages.

## Weekly operating cadence

- Add 15 manually qualified prospects per weekday.
- Send the first message only after the research checklist is complete.
- Follow up within one business day when a prospect replies or submits the audit form.
- Publish three focused LinkedIn posts and make five referral asks per week.
- Review the funnel every Friday: qualified prospects → positive replies → audit requests → discovery calls → proposals → won sales.
- Keep one audience and one core offer until at least 100 qualified prospects have produced enough evidence to change either.

These activity levels are planning assumptions, not guaranteed benchmarks. Adjust from observed conversion data.

## Lead tracking

Use the admin pipeline status consistently:

- `new`: form captured; not yet reviewed
- `qualified`: audience and need appear to fit
- `contacted`: a personal response or audit has been sent
- `discovery`: a sales conversation is scheduled or underway
- `proposal`: paid scope and terms have been sent
- `won`: paid engagement accepted and payment received
- `lost`: explicitly declined, unsuitable, or closed without a sale

Preserve source, medium, campaign, landing page, research note, last contact date, and next action. The campaign URL attribution persists from the landing page into the contact form.

## Decision rules

- Low opens: review deliverability, sender identity, subject lines, and list quality.
- Opens but few replies: improve the prospect observation and question.
- Positive replies but few audits or calls: simplify the offer and remove scheduling friction.
- Calls but few proposals: tighten qualification and make the pilot outcome concrete.
- Proposals but few wins: review urgency, trust, scope, proof, and pricing with actual objections.
- Never compensate for weak relevance by increasing bulk volume.

## Before outreach starts

- Choose and document the paid pilot scope, price, payment link/invoice process, and delivery capacity.
- Confirm domain email authentication and test delivery to major inbox providers.
- Review the outreach and privacy rules that apply to the prospect’s location and channel.
- Replace placeholders, test every campaign link, and personally review every first-touch message.
